\subsection{Evolution of Power Flow Models}

\subsubsection{Classical Models: Single-Node and Linearized DistFlow}
The development of energy management models for microgrids has historically relied on simplified power flow representations. The most fundamental approach is the single-node abstraction, which aggregates all generations and loads into a single bus \cite{Liu2022, Botelho2022}. While computationally trivial and suitable for high-level economic dispatch, this model fails to capture internal grid physics. By completely ignoring the network topology, it masks potential line overloads and internal voltage violations, leading to schedules that may be physically unrealizable when subjected to actual network constraints \cite{Feng2025, Samende2022}. To introduce spatial awareness without sacrificing linear solvability, the Linearized Distribution Flow (LinDistFlow) model was widely adopted \cite{Ullah2021, Feng2022}. This model simplifies the power flow equations by ignoring transverse voltage drops and assuming negligible line losses, thereby providing a purely linear approximation of power flows \cite{Patari2021, Pareek2020}. This linearity was highly attractive as it allowed the use of robust Mixed-Integer Linear Programming solvers. However, its accuracy severely deteriorates in heavily loaded networks or those with substantial reactive power flows. The lossless assumption becomes invalid when bidirectional power flows from high renewable energy penetration increase system losses, rendering it inadequate for modern active distribution networks where precise voltage regulation is mandatory \cite{Aboshady2023, Helou2021, Saidi2021, Xu2021}.

\subsubsection{Modern Model: Branch Flow Model and SOCP Relaxation}
To overcome the inaccuracies of classical approximations, the exact AC Optimal Power Flow (AC-OPF) modeled via the nonlinear Branch Flow Model (BFM) is required. This exact formulation precisely accounts for line losses, phase angles, and complex power flows. Nevertheless, the non-convex nature of AC-OPF, characterized by quadratic terms and trigonometric relationships, makes it computationally intractable and prone to trapping solvers in suboptimal local optima. Consequently, the Second-Order Cone Programming (SOCP) relaxation has emerged as the state-of-the-art methodology to bridge the gap between physical accuracy and computational feasibility \cite{Roald2022}. By relaxing the rigorous nonlinear equality constraint linking active power, reactive power, voltage, and current into a mathematically convex inequality cone, SOCP convexifies the entire problem \cite{Patari2021, Mahdavi2021}. This vital transformation guarantees that any local optimum found is also the global optimum, ensuring both global optimality and computational efficiency while rigorously respecting the underlying physics of radial distribution networks \cite{Fobes2020, Etedadi2021, Srivastava2023}. Under standard operating conditions—such as avoiding significant reverse power flows and maintaining sensible voltage limits—the SOCP relaxation is mathematically proven to be exact, meaning the relaxed inequality constraint naturally becomes strictly active at the optimal solution, yielding physically meaningful and exact AC results.

\subsection{Significance of Reactive Power and Voltage in Distribution Networks}

\subsubsection{Nonlinear P-Q-V Coupling in High R/X Ratio Networks}
Unlike transmission grids where the decoupling of active power-angle (P-$\delta$) and reactive power-voltage (Q-V) is a valid assumption due to high line reactance, distribution networks possess fundamentally different physical properties, primarily characterized by high resistance-to-reactance (R/X) ratios. In such resistive environments, active power flows cause significant voltage drops, inducing a strong, nonlinear coupling between active power ($P$), reactive power ($Q$), and voltage magnitudes ($V$) \cite{Hashmi2020, Amir2023}. This means that any fluctuation in active power generation from Distributed Energy Resources (DERs) or dynamic power exchanges in peer-to-peer (P2P) markets will simultaneously and directly impact local voltage profiles \cite{Polat2025}. Consequently, the traditional paradigm of separating real power scheduling from voltage control is no longer viable. Therefore, energy management systems cannot treat active power trading merely as an isolated economic variable; it must be continuously and rigorously evaluated against its reactive power implications to proactively manage network congestion and maintain strict voltage stability across the entire distribution network \cite{Gao2022}.

\subsubsection{Trade-off Between Sequential Coordination and Co-optimization}
Traditional energy management frameworks often employ sequential coordination: active power dispatch is solved first to prioritize purely economic minimization, followed by a secondary reactive power adjustment step designed to correct any resulting voltage deviations. This decoupled approach is fundamentally suboptimal because the initial active power dispatch may exhaust the capacity of inverters or push the network state too close to its limits, leaving insufficient margins for the subsequent reactive power adjustments. As a result, this often forces the curtailment of renewable generation or leads to unnecessarily high operational costs to restore grid stability \cite{Zhang2020Steady, Fazlhashemi2020}. In contrast, the joint co-optimization of $P$ and $Q$ explicitly integrates both power components into a single objective function and solves their operational constraints simultaneously. By deeply leveraging the often-underutilized reactive power capabilities of Battery Energy Storage Systems (BESS) and smart PV inverters, this holistic co-optimization approach maximizes the overall economic utility of physical assets. It effectively mitigates active power network losses and pre-emptively prevents voltage violations before they occur, proving fundamentally indispensable for the reliable and robust operation of modern multi-microgrid systems.

\subsection{Evolution of Grid Optimization Algorithms}

\subsubsection{Centralized Optimization and Privacy Barriers}
Historically, multi-microgrid energy management was orchestrated by a centralized Energy Management System (EMS), which actively gathers all detailed network parameters, real-time load demands, internal generator cost functions, and local physical constraints into a single computational hub \cite{Yuan2023, Chen2021}. While centralized solvers can theoretically and readily identify the true global optimum due to complete system visibility, they exhibit critical real-world vulnerabilities. The strict prerequisite of sharing highly sensitive operational data and proprietary commercial information fundamentally violates the data sovereignty and privacy expectations of independent microgrid operators, often deterring participation in cooperative frameworks \cite{Liu2025, Zhang2024}. Furthermore, a fully centralized architecture inherently suffers from severe scalability bottlenecks; as the number of active DERs, smart meters, and dynamic prosumers scales exponentially, the required communication bandwidth and the central computational burden rapidly become unsustainable. This heavy reliance on a central server also exacerbates system vulnerability, introducing a critical single-point-of-failure that could compromise the entire multi-microgrid network during cyber-attacks or communication outages \cite{Riedel2024, Zuo2022}.

\subsubsection{Decentralized Optimization: ADMM and the Significance of Dual Variables}
To resolve the inherent paradox between achieving global mathematical optimality and preserving local data privacy, decentralized optimization algorithms have become a paramount necessity in modern grid operations. Among various distributed techniques, the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) stands out due to its superior numerical convergence properties, robustness against communication delays, and overall architectural elegance compared to standard dual ascent methods \cite{Yuan2022, Zhao2022, Sun2024}. ADMM systematically decomposes the monolithic global coordination problem into smaller, mathematically independent subproblems that can be solved locally in parallel by each individual microgrid EMS. During this iterative process, the only information exchanged between neighboring entities is the expected power flow at their shared boundary nodes (the point of common coupling) alongside a corresponding dual variable, thereby ensuring the absolute privacy of all internal operational parameters and cost functions \cite{Chen2025, BASARAN2026}. Crucially, the dual variables utilized in ADMM are not merely abstract mathematical artifacts necessary for algorithmic convergence; they possess a profound physical and economic meaning. At the point of convergence, these dual variables intrinsically represent the Distribution Locational Marginal Price (DLMP) at the specific boundary nodes \cite{Ullah2022, Nasiri2023}. This endogenous DLMP organically acts as a decentralized, dynamic pricing signal that transparently reflects the true, real-time cost of energy provision. By encapsulating not only the marginal cost of generation but also the marginal costs associated with ohmic network losses and line congestion, DLMPs automatically penalize grid-stressing transactions and naturally incentivize energy trading that supports overall system stability \cite{Zhang2020, Xia2024, Zhu2024, Mishra2024}.
