\subsection{Problem Statement: Power System Transition and Energy Coordination Challenges}
In the context of global climate change and the depletion of fossil fuels, the traditional power system is undergoing a profound and comprehensive transition. Historically, power systems were designed around large, centralized fossil-fuel power plants that generated electricity and transmitted it over long distances to passive end-users. However, environmental concerns and technological advancements have catalyzed a paradigm shift. The massive penetration of Distributed Energy Resources (DERs)—such as solar photovoltaics (PV), wind turbines, and energy storage systems—has fundamentally altered the operational paradigm of conventional grids \cite{Muhtadi2021, Dagar2021}. Electricity consumers are no longer passive participants; they have transformed into prosumers capable of self-generating and injecting power back into the grid. This bidirectional flow of energy challenges the traditional unidirectional design of distribution networks, which were not originally built to handle dynamic, localized generation. Consequently, this decentralized evolution poses immense challenges in energy coordination. Integrating millions of DERs requires a flexible, decentralized management mechanism rather than traditional centralized dispatch, to prevent network congestion, manage bidirectional power flows, and avoid safety limit violations within the distribution grid \cite{Ahmad2023}.

\subsection{From Centralized Grids to Microgrids: The Role of Distributed Energy Resources}
To effectively manage the complexity introduced by DERs, the concept of the Microgrid (MG) has emerged as an essential structural solution \cite{She2022}. Unlike the macro-grid, which operates on a massive scale and is vulnerable to cascading failures, microgrids allow for the localized clustering of loads and distributed generators within a defined electrical boundary. They are capable of operating either in grid-connected mode, drawing or supplying power to the main grid, or in islanded mode, functioning autonomously during grid outages \cite{Trivedi2022}. These entities act as the fundamental building blocks of the Smart Grid, decentralizing energy management, significantly reducing transmission losses over long distances, and enhancing power supply reliability for local communities. Furthermore, the integration of Battery Energy Storage Systems (BESS) into microgrids effectively addresses the inherent intermittency and volatility of renewable energy sources by storing surplus generation during periods of high output and discharging it during peak load hours or times of low generation \cite{Choudhury2022}. As the number of microgrids proliferates across the distribution network, the operational challenge expands from managing a single-microgrid scope to ensuring the optimal coordination and stable operation of intricate, interconnected multi-microgrid systems.

\subsection{Peer-to-Peer (P2P) Energy Trading: Enhancing Economic Efficiency and Energy Autonomy}
Instead of relying solely on one-way transactions with utility companies via fixed, often rigid pricing mechanisms like Feed-in-Tariffs (FIT), Peer-to-Peer (P2P) energy trading introduces a disruptive and flexible economic model \cite{Victoria2021, Karami2021}. P2P trading empowers prosumers and microgrids to directly trade their surplus energy with one another through localized Energy Markets \cite{Ali2022}. This peer-driven approach democratizes the energy sector by bypassing traditional utility intermediaries, thereby reducing transaction overheads and empowering consumers. Recent literature demonstrates that, compared to conventional FIT mechanisms, P2P trading yields superior financial benefits for participants, significantly shortens the payback period for initial DER investments, and maximizes the overall social welfare of the participating community \cite{Park2024, Antonopoulos2020}. This mechanism not only creates direct economic incentives for sustainable energy investments but also fosters the energy autonomy and resilience of local communities \cite{Zia2020}. Nevertheless, realizing P2P trading in practice requires a highly robust mathematical and operational framework to effectively reconcile the economic objectives of diverse market participants with the stringent physical limitations and security constraints of the underlying grid infrastructure.

\subsection{Challenges in Multi-Microgrid Networks}

\subsubsection{Limitations of Single-Bus Microgrid Abstraction}
A critical flaw in the majority of existing literature on P2P energy markets is the widespread abstraction of an entire microgrid as a single electrical node (single-bus abstraction) or the assumption of a copper-plate network, where power can flow unhindered between any two points without losses or limits \cite{Liu2022, Botelho2022}. While computationally convenient, this approach completely neglects the underlying physical constraints of the practical distribution network, such as actual line resistances, inherently radial topologies, and strict branch power capacity limits. When purely economically driven P2P transaction schedules are physically dispatched over the real network, they frequently lead to severe network congestion, critical voltage limit violations, and potential equipment damage \cite{Feng2025, Samende2022}. Even when researchers attempt to address this fundamental flaw by utilizing linearized power flow models (e.g., LinDistFlow or DC-OPF), the inherent approximation errors can reach 5-10\% in systems with high renewable penetration, compromising the mathematical exactness required for safe and reliable grid operation \cite{Ullah2021, Feng2022}. Consequently, there is an urgent need to employ rigorous Second-Order Cone Programming (SOCP) relaxations. These advanced techniques transform the highly non-linear and non-convex AC Optimal Power Flow (AC-OPF) problem into a mathematically tractable convex form, guaranteeing global optimality while rigorously preserving the grid's true physical reality and operational safety margins.

\subsubsection{Importance of Joint Active-Reactive Power Co-optimization}
Modern distribution networks typically exhibit high resistance-to-reactance ($R/X$) ratios, distinguishing them from transmission systems. Consequently, effective voltage regulation within these localized grids is heavily dependent not only on reactive power ($Q$) but also substantially on active power ($P$) flows. Despite this physical reality, many existing P2P trading studies focus exclusively on active power optimization to maximize direct financial returns, completely disregarding the vital reactive power injection and absorption capabilities of the advanced smart inverters coupled with DERs and BESS \cite{Patari2021, Pareek2020}. Neglecting this crucial aspect severely diminishes the local voltage regulation capabilities, potentially leading to instability, and forces the system to forfeit valuable potential revenue from providing necessary ancillary services to the main grid. The joint, holistic co-optimization of both active and reactive power for BESS and distributed generators has been conclusively proven to yield significantly higher overall economic efficiency while robustly safeguarding the distribution grid against localized voltage sags and power quality degradation \cite{Aboshady2023, Xu2021, Hashmi2020, Amir2023, Fazlhashemi2020}.

\subsubsection{Privacy Requirements and Distributed Pricing}
Beyond the complex physical constraints of power flow, safeguarding data privacy represents arguably the most substantial non-technical barrier to the widespread scalability and adoption of interconnected multi-microgrid networks. Traditional Centralized Energy Management Systems (EMS) inherently require the continuous aggregation of highly sensitive operational information from all participating microgrids—including proprietary cost functions, internal network topologies, real-time load profiles, and specific BESS parameters—to a single, remote central server for optimization. This centralized architecture not only blatantly violates the operational privacy of independent microgrid operators but also creates a critical single point of failure, making the entire energy network highly vulnerable to malicious cyberattacks and data breaches \cite{Yuan2023, Chen2021}. To effectively overcome this critical vulnerability, the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) algorithm has been widely adopted due to its exceptional ability to preserve absolute data privacy. ADMM achieves optimal coordination by requiring only the iterative exchange of limited boundary power variables and price signals at the point of common coupling, without ever revealing internal parameters \cite{Liu2025, Riedel2024}. Furthermore, to accurately and fairly reflect the true operational cost of energy at each specific location—explicitly accounting for both physical network losses and potential line congestion—the Distribution Locational Marginal Pricing (DLMP) mechanism, which can be extracted endogenously directly from the ADMM dual variables, proves vastly superior to traditional, overly simplistic uniform pricing schemes \cite{Ullah2022, Nasiri2023, Zhu2024}.

\subsection{Research Motivation and Objectives}
A critical and systematic review of the current literature reveals a clear and persistent research gap: very few studies have successfully integrated a comprehensive, holistic framework that simultaneously encompasses (1) an exact and rigorous physical grid model via SOCP relaxation to ensure operational safety, (2) a fully distributed, privacy-preserving ADMM-based solver featuring dynamic penalty parameters for accelerated algorithmic convergence, and (3) an endogenous, transparent DLMP pricing mechanism specifically tailored for multi-microgrid systems with deeply integrated BESS \cite{Wang2022, Sanjab2022, Rajaei2021, Wang2023, Chen2022}.

The primary motivation of this thesis is to definitively bridge this identified gap by developing a robust, unified model for modern decentralized energy networks. The specific research objectives formulated to achieve this overarching goal are as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item To develop a comprehensive, mathematically rigorous energy management model for individual prosumers and microgrids utilizing the Branch Flow Model and SOCP relaxation techniques.
    \item To design a fully distributed, secure P2P trading algorithm based on the ADMM framework equipped with a dynamic penalty mechanism, thereby guaranteeing 100\% operational data privacy and improving computational efficiency.
    \item To propose and implement an endogenous DLMP mechanism seamlessly derived from the ADMM convergence process to transparently compensate participants for actual network losses and actively alleviate localized grid congestion.
    \item To extensively simulate, test, and validate the proposed algorithmic performance, critically evaluating both the tangible techno-economic impacts and the convergence stability on complex, interconnected multi-microgrid test systems under various operational scenarios.
\end{itemize}
