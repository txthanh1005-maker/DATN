\subsection{ATC Overview}
The operation of MMG (Multi-MG) systems entails a complex distributed coordination problem. Traditional centralized operations require MGs to transmit all their local, potentially sensitive information---such as load demand, renewable generation profiles, BESS constraints---to a central operator. This paradigm not only creates computational bottlenecks for large-scale systems but also severely compromises the privacy of independent MG operators.

To resolve these physical and informational challenges, the ATC framework is introduced. ATC is a distributed optimization technique originally developed for multidisciplinary design optimization, which perfectly aligns with the hierarchical structure of modern distribution networks. Figure \ref{fig:mmg_conceptual} illustrates the conceptual topology of this Multi-Microgrid (MMG) system, where individual MGs can seamlessly trade active power through physical tie-lines while coordinating with the Main Grid. By decomposing the global OPF problem, ATC divides the Multi-MG system into a two-level hierarchical architecture:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Coordinator Level (Upper Level):} DSO or Utility Grid serves as the central coordinator. The DSO assigns the optimal target variables $P_{target,ij,t}$ (conceptually acting as the ideal tie-line reference) to achieve P2P consensus.
    \item \textbf{Local MG Level (Lower Level):} Each individual MG operates as an independent autonomous entity. Utilizing the target signals provided by the coordinator, each local MG solves an SOCP problem to determine its optimal internal asset dispatch and the scheduled tie-line power exchange $P_{tie,ij,t}$.
\end{itemize}

Through an iterative exchange of limited boundary variables (the target power $P_{target,ij,t}$ and the tie-line response $P_{tie,ij,t}$) alongside penalty multipliers, the ATC algorithm guides the MMG system toward a globally optimal equilibrium without exposing any internal constraints or local asset states. For mathematical clarity regarding directional power flow, the active power traded via the tie-line connecting MG $i$ to MG $j$ at time $t$ is denoted as $P_{tie,ij,t}$.

\subsection{The Coordinator Problem (Utility Grid)}
At the top level of the ATC hierarchy, the Coordinator (Utility Grid) serves as the central authority to establish P2P consensus. The objective function of the coordinator is formulated to penalize the mismatch between the coordinator's target $P_{target,ij,t}$ and the previous iteration's optimal tie-line scheduled power $P_{tie,ij,t}^{*,k}$ submitted by the local MGs, subject to strict reciprocity ($P_{target,ij,t} + P_{target,ji,t} = 0$). The optimization SOCP problem is given by:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:ATC_Utility_Grid_Obj}
\begin{aligned}
\min \quad & \sum_{t \in \mathcal{T}} \sum_{(i,j) \in \mathcal{L}^{trade}} \left[ \lambda_{ij,t}^k \left( P_{tie,ij,t}^{*,k} - P_{target,ij,t} \right) \right. \\
& \left. \quad + \frac{\rho_{ij,t}^k}{2} \left( P_{target,ij,t} - P_{tie,ij,t}^{*,k} \right)^2 \right] \\
\text{s.t.} \quad & P_{target,ij,t} + P_{target,ji,t} = 0
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
By solving this optimization problem, the coordinator formulates the target variable $P_{target,ij,t}$ that the lower-level MGs must track, yielding the optimal explicit target $P_{target,ij,t} = \left( P_{tie,ij,t}^{*,k} - P_{tie,ji,t}^{*,k} \right) / 2$.

where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $P_{target,ij,t}$ is the decision variable representing the optimal trading target determined by the coordinator for the power flow from node $i$ to node $j$ at time $t$.
    \item $P_{tie,ij,t}^{*,k}$ is the fixed local tie-line power exchange from $i$ to $j$ scheduled by the lower-level MG at the current iteration $k$.
    \item $\lambda_{ij,t}^k$ is the linear Lagrange multiplier (shadow price) for active power trading at iteration $k$.
    \item $\rho_{ij,t}^k$ is the quadratic penalty multiplier at iteration $k$, strictly penalizing the inter-node deviation between the target and the local response.
\end{itemize}

After solving the optimal target $P_{target,ij,t}^{*, k}$ and receiving the updated $P_{tie,ij,t}^{*, k}$ from the lower-level MGs, the coordinator updates the penalty multipliers for the next iteration to drive the deviation to zero. The update equations for the multipliers ($\lambda$ and $\rho$) are defined as follows:
\begin{subequations} \label{eq:ATC_multiplier_updates}
\begin{align}
    \lambda_{ij,t}^{k+1} &= \lambda_{ij,t}^k + \rho_{ij,t}^k \left( P_{tie,ij,t}^{*, k} - P_{target,ij,t}^{*, k} \right) \label{eq:ATC_lambda_update} \\
    \rho_{ij,t}^{k+1} &= 
    \begin{cases} 
      \tau \rho_{ij,t}^k & \text{if } (r_{ij,t}^k)^2 > \mu (s_{ij,t}^k)^2 \\
      \rho_{ij,t}^k / \tau & \text{if } (s_{ij,t}^k)^2 > \mu (r_{ij,t}^k)^2 \\
      \rho_{ij,t}^k & \text{otherwise}
    \end{cases} \label{eq:ATC_rho_update}
\end{align}
\end{subequations}
where the primal residual (mismatch) $r_{ij,t}^k$ and the dual residual (speed) $s_{ij,t}^k$ are explicitly defined to measure the convergence as follows:
\begin{subequations} \label{eq:ATC_residuals}
\begin{align}
    r_{ij,t}^k &= P_{tie,ij,t}^{*,k} + P_{tie,ji,t}^{*,k} \label{eq:ATC_primal_residual} \\
    s_{ij,t}^k &= \rho_{ij,t}^k \left| P_{tie,ij,t}^{*,k} - P_{tie,ij,t}^{*,k-1} \right| \label{eq:ATC_dual_residual}
\end{align}
\end{subequations}
The balancing parameters are set as $\tau = 1.5$ and $\mu = 10$. These specific values are empirically tuned based on the network's impedance characteristics and operating bounds to ensure the monotonic convergence of the dual gap. This Adaptive Residual Balancing approach dynamically adjusts the penalty parameter to maintain a balance between primal and dual feasibility, significantly accelerating the convergence of the ATC algorithm.

\subsection{The Local MG Problem with ATC}
In the ATC architecture, MGs receive the updated penalty multipliers ($\lambda_{ij,t}^k, \rho_{ij,t}^k$) and the optimal reference target ($P_{target,ij,t}^{k-1}$) broadcasted by the central Coordinator (Utility Grid). To align their local schedules with the global consensus, each MG integrates an Augmented Lagrangian penalty function into its base optimization objective.

The base objectives of each MG, defined as $\mathcal{F}_{normal}^{OPF}$ for grid-connected modes and $\mathcal{F}_{emergency}^{OPF}$ for islanded states (as previously established in Chapter 2), are mathematically penalized for any inter-node discrepancy. The Augmented Lagrangian penalty function corresponding to the active power deviation along the tie-lines is formulated as follows \cite{ATC_Coopt_2025}:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:ATC_Penalty_Function}
\begin{aligned}
F_{penalty, i}^{ATC} &= \sum_{t \in \mathcal{T}} \sum_{j \in \mathcal{N}_i} \left[ \lambda_{ij,t}^k \left( P_{tie,ij,t} - P_{target,ij,t}^{k-1} \right) \right. \\
& \quad \left. + \frac{\rho_{ij,t}^k}{2} \left( P_{tie,ij,t} - P_{target,ij,t}^{k-1} \right)^2 \right]
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{N}_i$ represents the set of neighboring MGs directly connected to MG $i$.

The incorporation of this penalty effectively bridges the gap between the distributed operation and the global targets. The newly modified local ATC objective function, which each MG $i$ aims to minimize autonomously, is therefore defined as:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:ATC_Global_Objective}
\mathcal{F}_{ATC, i}^{OPF} = \mathcal{F}_i^{OPF} + F_{penalty, i}^{ATC}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{F}_i^{OPF}$ represents either $\mathcal{F}_{normal}^{OPF}$ or $\mathcal{F}_{emergency}^{OPF}$ depending on the prevailing operational condition of the network for MG $i$.

Consequently, the local problem is formulated as an SOCP model subject to its complete set of local operational and physical constraints defined in Chapter 2. Upon convergence of its local optimization, the MG transmits only the resulting scheduled tie-line active power exchange, $P_{tie,ij,t}$, back to the Utility Grid Coordinator for the subsequent ATC iteration.

\subsection{ATC Iterative Algorithm and Convergence}
\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Distributed ATC Algorithm}
\label{alg:atc_coordination}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\State \textbf{Initialization:} Set iteration counter $k \leftarrow 1$.
\State Initialize multipliers $\lambda_{ij,t}^1 = 0$, $\rho_{ij,t}^1 = \rho_0$ for all $(i,j) \in \mathcal{L}^{trade}, t \in \mathcal{T}$.
\State Initialize targets $P_{target,ij,t}^0 = 0$ and schedules $P_{tie,ij,t}^0 = 0$.
\Repeat
    \State \textbf{Lower Level: Local MG Optimization (Parallel Execution)}
    \For{each MG $i \in \mathcal{M}$}
        \State Receive global signals $P_{target,ij,t}^{k-1}$, $\lambda_{ij,t}^k$, $\rho_{ij,t}^k$ from the Coordinator.
        \State Solve the penalized local OPF minimizing $\mathcal{F}_{ATC, i}^{OPF}$ via \eqref{eq:ATC_Global_Objective}.
        \State Extract optimal schedule $P_{tie,ij,t}^k$ and transmit to the Coordinator.
    \EndFor
    \State \textbf{Upper Level: Coordinator Update}
    \State Calculate primal $r_{ij,t}^k$ and dual $s_{ij,t}^k$ residuals via \eqref{eq:ATC_residuals}.
    \State Update the optimal target $P_{target,ij,t}^k$ to enforce consensus.
    \State Update Lagrange multipliers $\lambda_{ij,t}^{k+1}$ via \eqref{eq:ATC_lambda_update}.
    \State Adjust penalty parameters $\rho_{ij,t}^{k+1}$ via Adaptive Residual Balancing \eqref{eq:ATC_rho_update}.
    \State $k \leftarrow k + 1$
\Until{$(r_{ij,t}^{k-1})^2 \le \epsilon_{stop}$ and $(s_{ij,t}^{k-1})^2 \le \epsilon_{stop}$ for 3 consecutive steps.}
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

ATC algorithm is an iterative process that alternates between solving the local MG optimization problems and updating the coordinator's target variables and penalty multipliers. The step-by-step procedure at iteration $k$ is detailed as follows:

\textbf{Step 1: Initialization.} Set the iteration counter $k = 1$. Initialize the Lagrange multipliers $\lambda_{ij,t}^1 = 0$, the penalty parameters $\rho_{ij,t}^1 = \rho_0$, the initial tie-line power schedules $P_{tie,ij,t}^0 = 0$, and the initial targets $P_{target,ij,t}^0 = 0$ for all directional tie-lines $(i,j) \in \mathcal{L}^{trade}$ and time intervals $t \in \mathcal{T}$.

\textbf{Step 2: Local MG Optimization.} Each MG autonomously solves its penalized OPF problem as defined in \eqref{eq:ATC_Global_Objective}, utilizing the target variables from the previous iteration $P_{target,ij,t}^{k-1}$. The optimal tie-line active power exchange $P_{tie,ij,t}^k$ is determined and subsequently transmitted to the Coordinator.

\textbf{Step 3: Coordinator Update.} Upon receiving the tie-line power schedules from all interacting MGs, the Coordinator updates the target variables, multipliers, and adaptive penalty parameters to enforce P2P consensus. 
First, the primal residual $r_{ij,t}^k$ and dual residual $s_{ij,t}^k$ are calculated to measure the inter-node mismatch and the algorithm's progress. Then, the target variable $P_{target,ij,t}^k$ is updated to drive the consensus. The Lagrange multipliers and adaptive penalty parameters ($\lambda_{ij,t}^{k+1}$ and $\rho_{ij,t}^{k+1}$) are sequentially updated according to the explicit rules defined in \eqref{eq:ATC_lambda_update} and \eqref{eq:ATC_rho_update} \cite{Mavromatis2021_AdaptiveRho}.

\textbf{Step 4: Convergence Check.} The iterative algorithm terminates when both the primal and dual residuals satisfy the convergence tolerance $\epsilon_{stop}$ (set to $10^{-4}$) for three consecutive iterations. If the criteria are met, the algorithm stops and the final optimal scheduling is achieved. Otherwise, the iteration counter is incremented ($k \leftarrow k+1$) and the process returns to Step 2.

\subsection{Summary of the ATC Decoupling Framework}

This chapter establishes the distributed coordination layer of the multi-microgrid system using analytical target cascading. By integrating an augmented Lagrangian penalty function with dynamic penalty scaling into local optimization models, the framework significantly accelerates convergence while enforcing global consensus. Operational privacy is strictly guaranteed via a minimal information exchange mechanism, as the architecture exclusively transmits boundary power and dual multipliers without revealing any internal physical grid parameters. Ultimately, this static hierarchical foundation securely orchestrates peer-to-peer energy trading and serves as the necessary prerequisite for integrating the model predictive control rolling-horizon mechanism in the subsequent chapter.
