\begin{sloppypar}
\begin{enumerate}[label=\Roman*.]
    \item Review the state-of-the-art in peer-to-peer (P2P) energy trading within
          multi-microgrid systems; analyze the role of distributed energy resources
          (DERs), physical grid constraints, and privacy requirements.
    \item Present the theoretical background on power flow models (from LinDistFlow
          to SOCP relaxation) and distributed optimization algorithms (ADMM).
    \item Develop a mathematical model for local microgrid energy management,
          including operational constraints for DGs, BESSs, renewable sources,
          and the objective function for cost minimization.
    \item Propose a distributed P2P coordination algorithm combining ADMM consensus
          with dynamic penalty and SOCP relaxation; derive the locational marginal
          price (LMP) from ADMM dual variables.
    \item Simulate and analyze results for two case studies (3-MG and
          4-MG cross-connected systems); evaluate economic performance, voltage
          regulation, network losses, and convergence behavior.
\end{enumerate}
\end{sloppypar}

\section*{ABSTRACT}
    \thispagestyle{empty}

The rapid penetration of renewable energy sources into distribution networks
has driven the emergence of microgrids (MGs) as a fundamental building block
of future smart grids. Multi-microgrid (Multi-MG) systems offer significant
potential for peer-to-peer (P2P) energy trading, enabling economic dispatch
and energy autonomy among prosumers. However, existing coordination frameworks
commonly adopt a single-bus MG abstraction, which neglects the physical
constraints of the distribution network---including voltage magnitude limits,
line thermal limits, and the nonlinear coupling between active power, reactive
power, and voltage (P-Q-V). Furthermore, centralized approaches compromise
the privacy of individual microgrid operators.

This thesis proposes a fully distributed P2P energy trading framework for
multi-microgrid systems that simultaneously addresses both the physical and
informational challenges. The framework integrates: (1) a \textbf{Second-Order
Cone Programming (SOCP) relaxation} of the Branch Flow Model (BFM) to
accurately capture multi-bus AC network physics within each microgrid, and
(2) an \textbf{ADMM (Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers) consensus
algorithm with a dynamic penalty parameter} to decompose the global optimization
problem into privacy-preserving local subproblems. A key feature of the
proposed approach is that the \textbf{Locational Marginal Price (LMP)} emerges
endogenously from the ADMM dual variables, providing a physically meaningful
and cost-reflective pricing signal without requiring a centralized market
operator.

Simulation results on two test cases---a three-microgrid radial system and a
four-microgrid cross-connected network---demonstrate that the proposed framework
achieves superior economic performance, maintains voltage profiles within
permissible bounds, minimizes network losses, and converges reliably. Compared
to single-bus and linearized (LinDistFlow) benchmarks, the SOCP-based approach
eliminates physically infeasible dispatch schedules and provides accurate
reactive power coordination.
